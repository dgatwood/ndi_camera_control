NDI PTZ Control Box Assembly

This is the documentation for how to build an NDI/VISCA PTZ Controller based on Raspberry Pi.  This documentation assumes at least a basic knowledge of electrical wiring and terminology.  The source code for the underlying software can be found at https://github.com/dgatwood/ndi_camera_control.

The software supports the following hardware controls/features, all of which are at least ostensibly optional:

  • A screen for showing the camera view.
  • A joystick for panning, tilting, and zooming the camera
  • Buttons for loading and storing preset positions
  • Status LEDs

When LEDs are present, they show whether the set button is engaged, whether you have just stored or retrieved a position using one of the preset buttons, and whether the camera is in preview mode, program mode (live) or malfunctioning.

When LEDs are not present, you can pass the -O flag to enable on-screen boxes that take the place of the lights in question.

Choosing a Flat Panel

Choosing a flat panel is tricky.  An ideal flat panel would use DSI for the video signal, would be powered by DSI, and would have PCB tabs that stick out above and below the panel through which you could run mounting screws.  Unfortunately, these panels do not currently exist as far as I can tell.

The flat panels currently on the market are substandard in various ways.  They all are either:

  • Designed to be powered by (or worse, controlled through) the multipin connector.
  • Panels powered through the 40-pin connector likely cannot be used, because the joystick, fan, buttons, and LEDs require most of the multipin connector to be free.  See Pin Use at the bottom.
  • Panels controlled through SPI are even more hopeless (for CPU consumption reasons; it’s all the Pi 4 can do to decode 1080p, and with some cameras you may have to downgrade to 720p.  Adding the SPI video overhead on top of that is a non-starter).
  • Designed to use HDMI, with connectors on the bottom and no way to mount the Pi to the board.  The connector location is challenging, and these panels require extra cables and adapters for power and HDMI.
  • Designed to use DSI, with power connectors that stick out one end.  The connector location is challenging, resulting in serious compromises, such as the inability to use a USB-C power supply.
  • Designed to use DSI, powered by the Pi, but mounted with screw holes in the back.  These are a pain in the backside to mount, but are otherwise awesome.

I’ll talk about these designs in the sections that follow, so that you can understand some of the pain points involved, and make the best decision possible.

HDMI Panels With Bottom Connectors

My first box was built with this panel, after eliminating dozens of other similar panels that would have taken up part or all of the 40-pin connector.

I chose to use an HDMI panel because I could potentially reuse it with devices other than the Raspberry Pi at a later date, if desired.  I encountered three big headaches with this design: Power, HDMI, and Pi mounting.

Providing this panel with power wasn’t nearly as simple as I expected, because of inadequate case clearance.  Powering the panel requires a USB cable plugged into the Pi.  Unfortunately, the Pimoroni sticks up a lot, which means you can’t mount it under the joystick.

To accommodate that, I mounted the Pin in such a way that the Pimoroni was underneath the panel.  Unfortunately, this put the USB connector too close to the end of the box.  I solved that problem, in turn, with a 180° USB adapter, which let me run the cable backwards across the top of the Pi and then up to the panel.  I plugged the cable provided with the panel into that adapter.  (This adapter might also be avoidable by turning the Pi by 180 degrees.)

I had to providing the panel with HDMI was also challenging, because there’s not enough space in that project box from front to back.  This caused problems on both ends of the HDMI cable — between the Pi and the box and between the panel itself and the box.

To solve that, I bought the following:

  • A right-angle micro-HDMI to HDMI cable (because there isn’t nearly enough space in that project box from front to back to have an HDMI cable sticking straight out the back of the Pi)
  • A flat HDMI ribbon with a 90-degree connector, which made it easy to connect to the flat panel.  (There’s very limited space between the panel and the front of the box, which makes that necessary.)

It may also be possible to use an HDMI 180° adapter (which may come with the panel) instead of the flat HDMI ribbon.  However, having two cables makes it easier to take it apart over and over again.

Also, there was not enough case clearance from top to bottom to mount the HDMI connector at the top and the buttons at the bottom.  To accommodate that, I mounted the panel upside down, with the HDMI connector at the bottom, and modified the Pi’s configuration file to rotate the screen by 180 degrees.

Finally, I had to mount the Pi somewhere.  The only really good spot is on the bottom of the case.  This involved the following extra parts and tools:

  • Plastic standoffs
  • Metric taps to make threaded holes in the bottom of the case

After installing the standoffs, I used a Dremel cutting wheel to carefully trim the tips of the standoff bolts (because they stuck out beyond the bottom of the box).

You can also use plastic nuts on the bottom and rubber feet, albeit with a slightly less attractive appearance.

DSI Panels with Power From the Side

My second design used this panel, which gets power from the side.  I strongly recommend that you avoid this design, because it is impossible to build a box with that panel unless you are willing to accept powering it from a USB 2.0 power supply.

The problems with this design are many:

  • The panel provides power to the Pi, rather than the other way around.
  • The power connector coming out the end runs into the joystick.  There’s not room for a traditional right-angle adapter (by a mile).
  • Moving the joystick down means that there’s no room for the Ethernet cable because of where the Pi mounts.
  • Moving the PI to one side is hard because it does not use 0.1” pins for its power and audio connectors.
  • The connectors are on the left end of the panel, and the joystick is on the right end of the panel.  This meant I had to mount this panel upside down and edit the Pi’s configuration file to rotate the screen by 180 degrees.

I solved the power problem crudely with a very small up-angle adapter.  Unfortunately, that adapter (along with all similar adapters and thin cables that I could find) does not pass USB-C power delivery signals, so you will not be able to power the device with any USB-C power supply if you use this panel, and will be relegated to USB 2.0 supplies via USB-A to USB-C cables.

Even with those ultra-thin adapters, space was at a premium, so I had to significantly cut into the corner support for the project box just to make things fit.

In theory, it is also possible to use a longer DSI cable and use the plastic that you removed for the screen to shift the mounting point for the Pi over far enough to make it work with normal USB-C power adapters, but this poses other challenges.  Specifically, they did not use standard 0.1” pin spacing on their connectors, so you’ll have a heck of a time moving the connectors, and will have to extend the USB-C cable itself, which is challenging enough that when I realized the width problem, I built a new top case with the corners cut out.

Note: It may be possible to power these DSI panels from the Pi.  I have not tried it.  IF that is possible, then this would be a great choice of panel.  However, I’m pretty sure that you can’t do that.

DSI Panels with Rear Screw Holes For Mounting

The final box that I built used a DSI panel that had screw holes on the underside, and drew power from the Pi — specifically, this panel.  This is my least un-favorite of the three panels.  However, the rear mounting holes posed a bit of a challenge.

To support this panel, I did the following:

  • Drilled holes above and below the panel, roughly adjacent to the threaded sockets on the back of the panel.  (I used the holes I had drilled for the previous end-powered DSI panels, but the hole position is unimportant for this panel design.)
  • Bought some plastic standoffs.
  • Fastened four of the shorter standoffs (about the second size from the bottom) to the underside of the lid with screws from the outside, so that the plastic part of the standoff sticks up.
  • Cut four small rectangular strips out of the big piece of plastic you removed, and drill holes so that the standoff goes through one hole, and the other hole is over top of the threaded sockets on the back of the panel.
  • Applied screws and nuts appropriately.

So basically, you had a screw coming in from the outside into the top of the standoff, followed by a small strip of plastic, followed by a nut.  At the other end of that strip of plastic was another hole, and I put a screw through that hole into the mounting hole on the back of the panel.

This was seriously a pain in the backside, and the fact that this is the least painful of the three designs to work with should tell you just how much of a pain the other two were.

Physical Construction

Once you’ve chosen a panel, the next thing to do is mount all of the hardware.  The basic box construction consists of five main tasks:

  • Mounting the flat panel
  • Mounting the buttons and LEDs
  • Mounting the fan
  • Mounting the joystick
  • Mounting the power and network connectors

These are described in the sections that follow.

Mounting the Flat Panel

  1. Cut a big hole with a dremel or jigsaw.
  1. As far left as possible.
  2. As high as possible.
  3. Leave room for mounting screw holes above it.
  1. Drill screw holes appropriately for mounting the panel.
  2. If necessary, build mounting tabs by cutting strips out of the cut-out piece.
  3. Screw everything together.

See photos below, grouped by the type of panel.

Mounting the Buttons and (Optional) LEDs

To mount the buttons and LEDs, drill a bunch of holes that are the right size for your buttons and LED holders.

The pushbuttons I linked to above are slightly bigger than 1/4“.  I just drilled a quarter-inch hole and then reamed it out by twisting the drill bit a little.  Ideally, you should use a 7mm bit (I think).

#OptionalLights: The LED holes should be sized based on the size of the LED holder.  For the LED holders I linked above, the 5mm LED holders take a 6.5mm hole, and the 3mm LED holders take a 6mm hole (but start smaller and work your way up).

You can mount the pushbuttons wherever you like. I mounted the set button on the back left corner (to avoid accidentally bumping it), and the other buttons across the bottom below the LCD.  You can also put them below the joystick if you prefer.

Important: If your flat panel has an HDMI connector and a power connector on the bottom, be sure to position the buttons in such a way that you can connect a cable to those connectors (typically with an up-angle/down-angle or 180-degree adapter or cable).

We’ll build the wiring harness later.

Mounting the Fan

Steps:

  1. Drill small pilot holes through the mounting holes in the fan, then drill them out with a larger bit.
  2. Cut slots with a Dremel cutting wheel for air flow.
  3. Drill some holes on the front near the bottom to let it pull air through the case.

See photos below.

Important: While you’re doing this, consider where the joystick will be and where the flat panel will be.

  • If you are mounting it too close to the joystick, you will want to make sure you mount the fan so that it is roughly touching the bottom of the box, because the joystick is pretty tall.  A right-angle drill attachment or Dremel Stylus can be very helpful here.
  • You can usually get away with mounting it almost anywhere under the flat panel, because the flat panel doesn’t stick much below the bottom of the top case, if at all, but measure first.

Mounting the Joystick

Before you mount the joystick, you have to figure out where to put it.

  • For HDMI panels, you can put it almost anywhere.  You’ll be mounting the Raspberry Pin on the bottom of the case, so there are few, if any clearance constraints as far as cables go.
  • For DSI panels, you’ll have to mount it as high up as possible, to make room for the ethernet cable coming out of that end of the Raspberry Pi.
  • For DSI panels with an end power connector (discouraged), you’ll also have to mount it as far to the right as possible, to leave room for the USB-C right angle adapter.

In general, I recommend mounting the joystick so that the pots are almost touching the side and top of the top case so that the joystick takes up as little space as possible.  (The corner sticks in, but you can nestle the pots into that wasted space beside and below that intrusion.)

If you have space, of course, you can also mount it the other way, if you prefer.

Once you have decided on a location, you can begin mounting the joystick.  To do this, first estimate the position of a single screw on the joystick from the underside as follows:

  1. Place the joystick flat-side-down on the inside surface of the top case.
  2. Draw a pencil mark out to the side that points towards one of the two holes that is farthest from the side of the box.
  3. Draw another pencil mark towards the bottom/top that points at the same hole.
  4. Remove the joystick, and extend the lines to see where they meet.
  5. Drill your first hole there.

Once you have the first hole, you can do the rest of the work from the outside by using the plastic ring that comes with the joystick:

  1. Fasten the ring to the outside of the box with a single screw (it can be loose; just keep it from moving around too much).
  2. Put a pencil in the other hole that is farthest from the side of the box, and make a mark by spinning the ring back and forth around the first screw.
  1. This creates a curved pencil mark that shows where the second screw hole could potentially be.
  1. Remove the ring and measure the distance from the side of the case to the first screw hole.
  2. Find the point on the line from step 7 that is that same distance from the side of the case.
  3. Drill your second screw hole there.
  4. Replace the ring and put screws in both holes.
  5. Mark and drill the remaining two holes in the usual way.
  6. Draw a circle on the inside of the ring, then remove the ring again.
  7. Cut out the center hole:
  1. Use a dremel to roughly remove the area inside the circle.
  2. Use a sanding wheel to finish the job.
  3. Periodically check the center hole against the ring to make sure you removed enough material.
  1. Insert the joystick, put the ring on the outside, and tighten the four screws.

This is pretty much the hardest part.

Mounting the Connectors

If the joystick was the hardest part, this is the most tedious.  Each connector mounts differently, so I’ve divided this into two parts.

Mounting the Ethernet Connector

The Ethernet connector is pretty straightforward to mount.  First, decide out where you’re going to put it.  It needs to be fairly low and out of the way, but otherwise, it can be pretty much anywhere.

Hold the connector in place, and take a very small drill bit and put it through the two screw holes in the connector.  Spin that bit with your fingertips.  Remove the bit and the connector, then drill where the marks are, using a larger bit.

Use a couple of drill bits or whatever to roughly hold the connector in place relative to the screw holes, then trace around the square part with a pencil.

Use a Dremel to trim out the square hole using an engraving tool.

Mounting the USB-C Connector

The USB-C connector is a bit harder to mount because it isn’t designed for thick cases.  To be brutally honest, I’d almost be tempted to use a USB-B panel-mount connector and adapters rather than this.  If anybody knows of any better, easier-to-mount USB-C panel-mount pass-through connectors, I’d love to hear about them.

To mount this connector, I did the following:

  1. Used a tiny drill bit through the mounting holes, spun my hand, to make a mark on the outside of the case.
  2. Drilled those holes.
  3. Used a couple of drill bits through the holes to hold it in place while drawing around the connector.
  4. Used the Dremel with an etching tool to cut the hole for the connector itself.
  5. Used the Dremel with a sanding tool to make the case thinner from the inside.

Eventually, I was able to get it thin enough that I could get the screws to grip properly and could make the connector actually snap in properly.

In hindsight, a metal project box might have been easier (albeit more dangerous in terms of throwing tiny bits of metal up into the air).

Electrical Wiring

The electrical wiring happened in two stages.  First, I built the input circuitry — the three-axis joystick analog inputs and the digital button inputs.  These features are supported by the Pimoroni I/O Expander.  Current designs use six buttons and three axes, for a total of nine inputs out of the 14 possible pins.  It would therefore be possible to add five more buttons if you are so inclined.

Wiring the 3-Axis Joystick

Before you begin, be sure that the zoom, when in the center, measures the highest resistance between the red and white wires, and that the resistance between the black and white wires drops low when you turn the zoom to the left.  If this is not the case, adjust where you connect those three wires as needed.

  1. Cut a group of socket (female) connector wires; the colors should be grey, white, black, brown, and red.
  2. Shorten the wires that come out of the side of the pot (the zoom wires).  The red and white wires need to reach the pot on the side that detects vertical motion (the top-down pot).  The black wire just needs to reach the wiring harness.  You will use the cutoffs in the next two steps, so measure the cut-off pieces accordingly.
  3. Attach the cut-off red wire between the two visible pots.  It should be on the left side of the left-right pot as you face the box from the top, and towards the top of the up-down pot.
  4. Attach the cut-off black wire between those pots, from the right side to the bottom side.
  5. Attach the white bus cable wire to the center of the left-right pot.
  6. Attach the red and black bus cable wires to the two sides of the left-right pot to match the other wires.
  7. Attach the grey bus cable wire to the center of the up-down pot.
  8. Attach the brown bus cable wire to the black wire coming from the zoom.
  9. Solder everything EXCEPT for the black wire contact on the up-down pot (the one that connects to the other black contact).  Leave this unsoldered for now.
  1. We will eventually add another wire (green in photo #3 below) to go down to the buttons from here.
  2. If desired, you can attach a single-pin connector to this now and use a matching connector on the buttons.

Wiring the Main Buttons

The main buttons are typically red, yellow, green, blue, and black, and are mounted below the flat panel on the top case.

  1. Cut a group of socket (female) connector wires; the colors should be orange, yellow, green, blue, and purple.
  2. Insert the buttons in order: Red-yellow-green-blue-black.
  3. Connect one pin of each button to the next button with a black wire (or whatever is handy).  Also connect the last button to the black wire contact on the up-down pot that you left unsoldered in the previous step.  Solder these ground wires.
  4. Connect each button to the matching color of wire, substituting orange for red and purple for black.  Solder these wires.

Wiring the Set Button

The set button is typically white, and mounted on the back of the larger part of the project box.

  1. Cut a pair of socket header wires and solder them to the button.
  2. Take a pair of pin-to-socket wires and set them aside for connecting to the main bus connector.

Assembling the Main (Button/Joystick) Bus Connector

The main bus connector is shown below.

To assemble the bus connector:

  1. Remove all of the single plastic socket housings by lifting up the little plastic tab on one side with a screwdriver or other sharp implement.  It will break (or at least permanently deform).  Don’t worry about it.  You’re throwing away these housings.
  2. Insert the individual sockets into the new 10x2 housing one at a time, being sure to line up the side with the large indentation exactly with the side of the housing that has the spring pin.

The pinout of the Pimoroni is as follows:

+ 2 4 6 8 10 12 14 G2 G4

V 1 3 5 7  9 11 13 G1 G3

Where V is Vin (unused), G is ground, and + is Vout.

These are connected as follows:

+ : Red (to pots)

V : NC

1 : [Unknown color: To one wire of set button]

2 : Orange [red button]

3 : Yellow [button]

4 : Green [button]

5 : Blue [button]

6 : Purple [black button]

7 - 10 : NC

11: White (left-right pot center)

12: Grey (up-down pot center)

13: Brown (zoom pot center)

14: NC

G1 - G2 : NC

G3 : Black (to pots)

G4 : [Unknown color: To other wire of set button]

Note that the other end of the set button wire should be a set of pins to plug into the sockets that you previously soldered to the set button.  That way, you can remove the top without breaking wires.

If you’re being really clever, use pins on the set button, and a socket-to-socket pair on the other side, so that you won’t get the two pins going into the fan confused with the pins going into the set button.  (Doing so will cause your Pi to shut off instantly when you push the set button.  Ask me how I know.)

The Pimoroni requires you to solder a right-angle header onto the board, then plug it into the first five pins on the inner (board) side.  See the documentation for details.  Then, plug the bus connector into that.  (You can see this in the last photo above.)

#OptionalLights: Assembling the Light Bus Connector

To simplify use, this software also provides a series of lights on the front:

  1.  An RGB (common cathode) or two-color (common cathode) for the tally light (VISCA required):
  1. Red when the camera is on the program bus
  2. Green when the camera is on the preview bus
  3. Off when the camera is not on any bus
  4. Blue if communication with the camera has been interrupted
  1. A single LED for each of the five position buttons:
  1. On when that position is selected
  2. Rapidly blinking, then on when the position is set
  1. A single LED for the set button (on when pressed)

When you press the set button down, the set light illuminates as a visual confirmation.  When you press the set button again, set mode turns off, along with the light.  When a button is programmed, that light flashes repeatedly before becoming lit continuously.  When a button is triggered, that light is lit continuously until you press another button or move the joystick.

This requires another wiring harness.  For simplicity, this harness should be attached to the pins at the opposite end of the Raspberry Pi from the Pimoroni.  I used a 10x2 header cut down to 6x2 on the bottom twelve pins.  Ideally, you should use a real 6x2 header.

The pins are as follows:

29 GPIO 5 - White LED  | 30 Ground - White LED

31 GPIO 6 - Red LED    | 32 GPIO 12 - Black (purple) LED

33 GPIO13 - Yellow LED | 34 Ground - RGB LED

35 GPIO19 - Green LED  | 36 GPIO 16 - RGB LED Red

37 GPIO26 - Blue LED   | 38 GPIO 20 - RGB LED Green

39 Ground - Main LEDs  | 40 GPIO 21 - RGB LED Blue

For ease of debugging, I crimped my own female pins onto an RGBK cable that I had for LED lighting purposes, but you can use whatever.  This means that unlike some of the other photos, the light wires all match the light colors, with a black wire used for the ground.

The light pins should be connected to one end of an appropriate resistor on its way to the long pin of the appropriate LED.  The short pins should be connected to ground.  Use heat shrink tubing.

The exact resistor values will vary depending on the forward voltage and current of the LEDs, so you’ll need to use an online calculator to confirm the values, but the pack that I picked up need the following values:

  • White: 5–15Ω
  • Warm white: 5–15Ω
  • Red: 55–65Ω
  • Yellow: 55–65Ω
  • Green: 5–15Ω
  • Yellow-green: 55–65Ω
  • Blue: 5–15Ω
  • Orange: 55–65Ω
  • Purple: 15–0Ω (Test individually)
  • Pink: 5–15Ω

So I went with the nearest values that were quickly available: 68Ω and 18Ω.

For my RGB LEDs, the red side had the same specs as the individual red LEDs, and the blue and green had the same specs as the individual blue and green LEDs, so I used the same resistors.

Putting it all together

After you plug in all of the cables and screw everything together, what remains is the software.  You can get the software at https://github.com/dgatwood/ndi_camera_control.

Pin Use

For reference, this project uses the following pins:

  • Pins 1, 3, 5, 7, and 9 (I2C pins): Used for Pimoroni I/O Expander.
  • This could alternatively be moved to pins 17, 15, 16, 19, and 20, or various other combinations.  This is left as an exercise for the reader.
  • Pins 17 and 20: Used for fan power.  I’m using a 5V fan running at 3.3V (quieter), and this is the only place where 3.3V and ground are close enough together to be practical other than pins 1 and 6 (and pin 1 is used for the Pimoroni).
  • Pins 29 through 40: Used to drive the (#OptionalLights) status LEDs.
